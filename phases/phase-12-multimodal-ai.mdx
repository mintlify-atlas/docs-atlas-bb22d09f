## Multimodal representations

Different modalities live in different spaces. Text is a sequence of discrete tokens. Images are continuous pixel grids. Audio is a waveform sampled at 44kHz. Video is frames over time. A multimodal system must map all of these into a shared representation space where semantically related content — a photo of a dog and the word "dog" — is close together.

Three approaches to cross-modal alignment:

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Contrastive learning" icon="intersect">
    Train separate encoders for each modality and align their output spaces by pushing matching pairs together and non-matching pairs apart. Used by CLIP, ALIGN, and AudioCLIP. Simple, scalable, and produces general-purpose embeddings.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Generative alignment" icon="wand-magic-sparkles">
    Train a model to generate one modality conditioned on another. An image captioner learns vision-language alignment as a byproduct of generating accurate captions. Used by BLIP, Flamingo, and CogVLM.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Unified tokenization" icon="grid">
    Tokenize all modalities into a shared discrete vocabulary — images as VQ-VAE tokens, audio as codec tokens, text as BPE tokens — and train a single transformer on the combined stream. Used by Gato, GPT-4o, and Gemini.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## CLIP: Connecting vision and language

CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training, Radford et al. 2021) is the model that made zero-shot image classification practical. It trains a vision encoder and a text encoder jointly on 400 million (image, caption) pairs scraped from the web.

The training objective is simple: for a batch of N (image, text) pairs, maximize the cosine similarity of the N correct pairs and minimize the similarity of the N² − N incorrect pairs.

```
         ┌─────────────┐
images ──▶ Vision      ├──▶ image embeddings ──┐
         │ Encoder     │                        │   cosine
         └─────────────┘                        ├── similarity
                                                │   matrix
         ┌─────────────┐                        │   (N × N)
captions▶ Text         ├──▶ text embeddings  ──┘
         │ Encoder     │
         └─────────────┘
```

At inference, CLIP needs no task-specific labels. To classify an image, encode it and encode a list of candidate class names ("a photo of a cat", "a photo of a dog", ...). The class whose text embedding is most similar to the image embedding wins. This works for any class you can describe in natural language — even classes the model has never seen.

CLIP's learned embedding space enables downstream tasks beyond classification:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Image-text retrieval" icon="magnifying-glass">
    Encode a corpus of images and a query string, then retrieve the most similar images by cosine similarity. The foundation of multimodal search engines.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Semantic image clustering" icon="object-group">
    Cluster image embeddings without labels. CLIP embeddings group semantically similar images even when they look visually different.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Prompt-guided generation" icon="paintbrush">
    Stable Diffusion uses a CLIP text encoder to condition image generation. The text embedding directly guides what the diffusion model produces.
  </Card>
  <Card title="CLIP score evaluation" icon="star">
    Measure alignment between generated images and their text prompts. Higher CLIP score = the image better matches the description. Standard metric for evaluating text-to-image models.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Vision-language models

VLMs extend large language models to accept images as input alongside text. The architecture bridges the vision and language towers:

```
Image ──▶ Vision Encoder ──▶ Projection Layer ──▶ ┐
                                                    ├──▶ LLM ──▶ Text
Text tokens ────────────────────────────────────▶ ┘
```

The vision encoder (typically a ViT) produces a sequence of patch embeddings. The projection layer — a linear layer or a small MLP — maps these into the LLM's embedding space. The LLM then attends over both image tokens and text tokens jointly.

Key models and their design choices:

| Model | Vision encoder | Projection | LLM backbone |
|-------|---------------|------------|--------------|
| LLaVA | CLIP ViT-L/14 | Linear | Llama / Vicuna |
| InternVL | InternViT-6B | MLP | InternLM / Llama |
| Qwen-VL | ViT-bigG | Cross-attention | Qwen |
| Flamingo | NFNet | Perceiver resampler | Chinchilla |
| GPT-4o | Undisclosed | Undisclosed | GPT-4 class |

The projection layer is the key engineering decision. A simple linear projection is efficient but limits representational capacity. A cross-attention mechanism (as in Flamingo) is more powerful but adds latency. Most open-source VLMs use a two-layer MLP as the sweet spot.

## Audio-language models

Audio-language models process speech, music, or environmental sounds alongside text. The pipeline mirrors the vision-language approach:

```
Audio ──▶ Audio Encoder ──▶ Adapter ──▶ LLM
```

Two dominant audio encoders:

- **Whisper encoder**: extracts speech features from log-mel spectrograms. Pre-trained on 680,000 hours of speech. Strong for speech recognition tasks.
- **EnCodec / Codec2**: encodes audio as discrete tokens using a neural codec. Enables the LLM to generate audio as a sequence of tokens.

Models in this space: Qwen-Audio, WavLLM, AudioPaLM, Phi-4-audio. The binding capability — asking the model questions about audio content — is the primary use case for lesson 04.

## Document understanding

Document understanding combines vision and NLP to process documents that cannot be parsed as raw text: scanned PDFs, forms, tables, charts, and slides.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Layout-aware models" icon="file-text">
    Models like LayoutLM and DocFormer process the visual layout of a document (bounding boxes, font sizes, spatial positions) alongside the text content. A table cell gets different treatment than a heading.
  </Card>
  <Card title="OCR-free models" icon="eye">
    Models like Donut and Pix2Struct process the document as an image directly, bypassing OCR entirely. More robust to noisy scans and unusual layouts; slower than OCR-based approaches.
  </Card>
  <Card title="VLM-based extraction" icon="wand-sparkles">
    Send document pages as images to a vision-language model (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini) with a structured extraction prompt. Flexible but expensive for high-volume pipelines.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Hybrid pipelines" icon="layers">
    Combine OCR for text extraction with VLMs for layout understanding. Use heuristics to decide when to escalate to VLM processing (low OCR confidence, complex tables, charts).
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Video-language models

Video adds the temporal dimension: a sequence of frames over time. The core challenge is that processing every frame is computationally prohibitive — a 1-minute video at 30fps is 1,800 frames.

Practical approaches:

- **Sparse frame sampling**: sample frames uniformly or based on scene cuts. Works for slow-moving content; misses fast events.
- **Temporal pooling**: encode all frames with a vision encoder, then pool the frame embeddings over time. Efficient but loses temporal order.
- **Video transformers**: treat frames as a spatiotemporal volume and apply 3D attention. Accurate but expensive (Video-LLaMA, PLLaVA).

The lesson covers Video-LLaMA-style architectures and common downstream tasks: video captioning, temporal grounding ("at what timestamp does X happen?"), and video question answering.

## Multimodal RAG

Multimodal RAG retrieves both text chunks and images to answer queries. The index holds heterogeneous content:

```
Query ──▶ Embed ──▶ Search mixed index ──▶ Retrieve text + images
                                      ──▶ Augment VLM context
                                      ──▶ Generate answer
```

The key challenge is embedding heterogeneous content into a shared retrieval space. CLIP embeddings work naturally here — you can embed both text queries and images into the same space and compute similarity across modalities.

A production multimodal RAG pipeline:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Index documents">
    Extract text chunks, images, and tables from source documents. Embed text with a text encoder (e.g., `text-embedding-3-small`). Embed images with a vision encoder (e.g., CLIP ViT-L/14). Store all embeddings in a vector database with a `modality` field.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Query routing">
    Embed the user query with both text and vision encoders. Retrieve top-k results across both modality indexes, then merge with Reciprocal Rank Fusion.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Context assembly">
    Interleave retrieved text chunks and images in the VLM context window. Text chunks appear as text; images appear as image tokens.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Generate response">
    Send the augmented context to a VLM (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, or Qwen-VL). The model attends over both text and image context to produce a grounded answer.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Multimodal agents

Multimodal agents can perceive their environment visually and take actions based on what they see. This unlocks:

- **GUI automation**: an agent that reads screen content as images and clicks UI elements
- **Visual QA pipelines**: agents that retrieve images, reason over them, and answer questions
- **Robotics perception**: agents that interpret camera feeds to plan and execute physical actions

The lesson builds a multimodal agent loop that: receives a task, captures a screenshot (or retrieves an image), encodes it with a VLM, plans the next action, and executes it — repeating until the task is complete.

## Text-to-image pipelines

Lesson 09 covers the pipeline from text prompt to generated image, focusing on the production aspects of deploying diffusion-based systems:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Stable Diffusion pipeline" icon="image">
    CLIP text encoder → latent diffusion model (U-Net denoising) → VAE decoder. The latent space operates at 8× lower resolution than the output image, making generation feasible on consumer hardware.
  </Card>
  <Card title="ControlNet conditioning" icon="sliders">
    Add spatial conditioning (edge maps, depth maps, pose skeletons) to guide the generation. A ControlNet processes the conditioning signal and injects activations into the U-Net at each denoising step.
  </Card>
  <Card title="LoRA for style transfer" icon="palette">
    Fine-tune a small LoRA adapter on a target style (10–20 images). The adapter shifts the model's output distribution toward the target style without retraining the full model.
  </Card>
  <Card title="SDXL and beyond" icon="sparkles">
    SDXL uses a two-stage pipeline: a base model generates a low-resolution latent, a refiner model upscales it. FLUX.1 replaces the U-Net with a rectified flow transformer for higher quality and faster inference.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Text-to-video pipelines

Text-to-video extends diffusion to the temporal dimension. Current architectures:

- **Video diffusion models**: extend 2D U-Nets with temporal attention layers. Each frame is generated jointly, maintaining consistency across the video.
- **Autoregressive video**: generate frames one by one conditioned on previous frames. Simpler but accumulates error over long sequences.
- **Latent video diffusion**: operate in a compressed latent space (as Stable Video Diffusion does), then decode to pixels. Reduces memory from O(T × H × W) to O(T × h × w) where h, w are the compressed dimensions.

Key models: Stable Video Diffusion (Stability AI), Sora (OpenAI, closed), Wan2.1 (Alibaba, open), CogVideoX.

## Any-to-any models

The final lesson surveys models that unify all modalities in a single architecture and can map any modality to any other: text-to-image, image-to-text, audio-to-text, text-to-audio, and combinations thereof.

Representative architectures:

| Model | Approach | Input modalities | Output modalities |
|-------|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Gato (DeepMind) | Multi-task transformer with unified token vocabulary | Text, images, actions, observations | Text, actions |
| GPT-4o | Undisclosed unified architecture | Text, images, audio | Text, images, audio |
| Gemini 1.5 Pro | Multi-stream transformer | Text, images, audio, video | Text |
| ChameleonMix | Discrete token unification | Text, images | Text, images |
| MetaVoice-1B | Speech-text unified model | Text, audio | Audio |

The trend is toward models that share a single transformer backbone across all modalities, using modality-specific encoders and decoders at the boundaries. This allows cross-modal reasoning — the model can compare image regions to audio segments to text passages in the same attention computation — rather than passing representations across separate models.
